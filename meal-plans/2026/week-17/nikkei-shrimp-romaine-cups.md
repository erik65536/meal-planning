# Nikkei Shrimp Romaine Cups with Cauliflower Chaufa Rice

Peruvian-Japanese fusion: soy-lime glazed shrimp in aji amarillo sauce served warm over cauliflower chaufa rice — a riff on Peruvian fried rice — spooned into crisp romaine heart leaves. Bold, layered, and unlike anything else in the rotation. ~380 calories.

## Ingredients

### Shrimp

- 6–8 oz large shrimp (31/40 count), peeled and deveined, tails off
- 1.5 tbsp aji amarillo paste (substitute: 1 tsp sriracha + 0.5 tsp turmeric if unavailable)
- 1 tbsp low-sodium soy sauce
- 1 tbsp fresh lime juice
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- 2 cloves garlic, minced
- 0.5 tsp fresh ginger, grated
- 0.5 tsp avocado oil

### Cauliflower Chaufa Rice

- 2 cups riced cauliflower (from 1 head — use the rest for other meals this week)
- 2 medium carrots, julienned or cut into thin matchsticks
- 2 cloves garlic, minced
- 0.5 tsp fresh ginger, grated
- 1 tbsp low-sodium soy sauce
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- 0.5 tsp avocado oil
- 1 egg, lightly beaten [Optional, for richness]

### Cups & Garnish

- 6–8 large romaine heart leaves, separated and washed (inner leaves work best — firm enough to hold filling)
- 2 tbsp fresh cilantro, roughly chopped
- 2 green onions, thinly sliced
- 1 tbsp sesame seeds
- Lime wedge
- Extra aji amarillo paste or sriracha for heat [Optional]

**Prep Notes:**

- Pat shrimp completely dry before cooking — moisture causes steaming instead of searing. Dry shrimp = better color and texture.
- Marinate the shrimp in the aji amarillo, soy, lime, sesame oil, garlic, and ginger for 10–15 minutes while you prep the cauliflower rice. Don't marinate longer than 30 minutes — the lime juice will start to cook the shrimp.
- Rice the cauliflower using a box grater or food processor. The texture should be dry and loose — if it's wet, spread on a towel and press out excess moisture before cooking.
- Use the firm inner romaine leaves as cups — they hold their shape better than the outer leaves.
- Cook shrimp last so they go into the cups hot.

## Cooking Instructions

1. **Marinate the shrimp.** Combine aji amarillo paste, soy sauce, lime juice, sesame oil, garlic, and ginger in a bowl. Add shrimp, toss to coat, and set aside 10–15 minutes.

2. **Cook the cauliflower chaufa rice.** Heat 0.5 tsp avocado oil in a large skillet over medium-high heat. Add carrots and cook 2–3 minutes until just beginning to soften. Add garlic and ginger and cook 1 minute. Add riced cauliflower and spread into a single layer — let it sit undisturbed 1–2 minutes to get a little color. Stir, then season with soy sauce and sesame oil. If using egg: push cauliflower to the edges, pour egg into the center, scramble until just set, then fold into the rice. Taste and adjust soy. Transfer to a bowl and keep warm.

3. **Sear the shrimp.** Wipe out the skillet and heat 0.5 tsp avocado oil over high heat. Lift shrimp from the marinade, shaking off excess, and add in a single layer. Cook 1–2 minutes per side until pink, curled, and lightly charred at the edges. Pour any remaining marinade into the pan in the last 30 seconds and toss to glaze. Remove from heat immediately — overcooked shrimp is rubbery.

4. **Assemble.** Spoon cauliflower chaufa rice into each romaine cup. Top with 1–2 shrimp per cup. Spoon any remaining glaze from the pan over the top.

5. **Garnish and serve.** Scatter cilantro, green onions, and sesame seeds over the cups. Squeeze lime over everything. Serve immediately with extra aji amarillo or sriracha on the side.

## Notes

- **Calories**: ~380 total (shrimp ~185, cauliflower rice ~60, carrots ~40, egg ~70, oil ~25) — without egg ~310 cal.
- **Aji amarillo paste**: has a fruity, medium heat with a slightly floral quality distinct from other chilis. Mae Ploy or Goya brands are widely available; La Preferida and La Costeña are also good. Worth having in the fridge — it keeps for months and works in sauces, marinades, and dressings.
- **Chaufa**: Peruvian fried rice developed from Chinese Cantonese immigration in the 1800s. The Nikkei (Japanese-Peruvian) version incorporates soy, sesame, and ginger more prominently. Using cauliflower rice keeps this light without losing the character.
- **Romaine cups**: serve immediately — the warm filling wilts the romaine quickly if it sits.
- **Cauliflower**: 1 head produces far more than 2 cups riced. Use the rest across other meals this week (roasted florets as a side, added to the stir-fry, or blended into soup).
